This topic is so hot and crazy in the media right now, mainstream media and social media. So please know I'm not coming here to troll or stir the pot. I'd like to ask about vaccinating from an unschooling perspective as it pertains to older children.

How do you all handle this?

Setting aside the controversial issues about vaccines, some of these diseases can affect adults more seriously than when caught as a child.

But, in the meantime, what are the chances your son will get one of these disease before he's an adult? Virtually nil.

So since most people are vaccinated, you do have the luxury of letting him decide to get vaccinated or not. If he doesn't vaccinate before he's an adult, make information available for him so he can make an informed decision then. He'll need to know what vaccinations he's had and that immunity can fade. And about the diseases he could also be vaccinated for and what the effects might be if the diseases are caught as an adult.

Janice Ancheta at 2015-02-13 19:44:40 +0200 said:

Disclosure: I am a physician, but as a radiologist I do not give vaccines or prescribe medications, and I am not connected to any pharmaceutical companies. I approach this topic as a concerned mother and unschooler who happens to have a scientific background.

Here is my take on medicine from an unschooling perspective: I would not want to force a medication or procedure on a child (or adult, really) unless there is danger to himself or others AND the child is unable to comprehend the situation AND creative thinking leaves no other alternatives. I cannot say here what the right answer is in this or any other individual situation.

As Joyce said, you can make information available to your son. Most people will agree that it is important to be informed. But when it comes to vaccines, if you look for information by doing a google search, there is a good chance you and your son will be misinformed. The internet and even some books are filled with folklore, half-truths, and flat out lies about vaccines. There are even whole websites and organizations designed to look official and scientific, but are actually all smoke and mirrors. But it’s hard to tell unless you have a solid understanding of epidemiology and immunology, and you click on all the links and references on these sites.

In the United States, go straight to the CDC website http://www.cdc.gov/vaccines/pubs/parents-guide/parents-guide-part4.html. No organization (including the CDC) is perfect, but it’s the best source of actual data here. Discuss anything you don’t understand or any frightening rumors you hear with a trusted physician or other expert in the field.

I am not saying this to you specifically, but to everyone as a source for discussion. I think you move away from unschooling when you hold onto beliefs that are not supported by evidence, then impose these beliefs on your children as truths. Don’t believe whatever you read on the internet without checking sources. Don't believe that you are “educated” on a topic by reading a blog post while ignoring a large body of evidence. Experts can be wrong, but if you are going to say so, please be able to give real evidence as to why you think they are wrong.

I think if anyone really wants to learn about vaccines, study immunology, virology, epidemiology, anatomy and physiology, public health, etc. If not, see the CDC website and talk to people who are experts in these subjects. Don’t rely on internet lore.

Janice

heatherpie@... at 2015-02-13 19:52:46 +0200 said:

=My 9 year old is not fully vaccinated, and he's really resistant to the idea of getting shots. =

When Austin was 12 he was scared to get the shot, but interested in getting the vaccines recommended by our doctor. We went from there and found a way to make the shot less scary. Numbing the area with ice worked for him. I made him an ice pack wrapped in a towel and he held it on his arm on the way to the appointment and while we waited. He held my hand, looked away when it was time and was surprised when it was over that he hadn't felt pain. 

On a side note: Austin needs to get his blood drawn often and I am surprised when I see little kids waiting to get their blood drawn with no numbing of the area done. Ice is great for shots because it can numb down into the muscle where vaccines usually go. Lidocaine cream is great for blood draws as it completely numbs the area. It doesn't go far enough down to numb for a subcutaneous shot, but for blood draws it works wonderfully. 

Heather

What I picture in that scenario is a child approaching the parent, wanting to know if it's true, feeling afraid or self-righteous, and I would use that as an opportunity to discuss the issues involved in that idea. Why would someone say that? What are the concerns underneath that?

This is an area where are kids are likely to start out biased towards our view as the parent. Whenever my nine yo asserts something that is the product of *my* reasoning, rather than his, I try to offer some of the other perspectives.
