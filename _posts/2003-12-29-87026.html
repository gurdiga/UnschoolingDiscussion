I agree.

One of the closest, most loving families I know is my husband's uncle's
family, and he was an assembly-line worker for Ford up in Detroit for his entire
career, as is his son now. The regular hours and guaranteed income were ideal
for the stability of their lifestyle, and they had regular vacations at an
upstate cabin, had a boat, the works. All very middle-class, nothing
high-falutin', but it was good. I've never known such an idyllic family (although they
certainly had their problems, just like anyone else).

I always thought that the work must have been mind-numbing for the uncle, but
the quality of his family life was so high that I figure it must have been
worth it.

There have been times, as we have struggled to find a stable financial place
in the world, where I have envied him his humdrum, mind-numbing choice of a
blue collar factory job. :-)

Laura B.

The best job I ever had for providing me the ability to think was the most
boring and routine. So I could do what I was doing and think about other things
without any possibility of screwing up. It was a job without words, so words
and ideas could flow without conflict. It was taking dishes off trays in the
college dining hall. Each of us on that line had one type of dish to remove,
rinse, and put in the trays for the dishwashing machine. If I was doing
plates, I scanned for plates, rinsed and lined them up until the rack was full,
at which point I scooted that over and started another rack. It was WONDERFUL.
There was warm running water in a stainless steel channel I would hold my
hands in during lulls, the room was so loud we couldn't carry on conversations
anyway, and it was like being alone in a meditative state.
